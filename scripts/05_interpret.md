# Phase 5 — Interpretation of Findings

**Audience:** Leadership team, Futuro Digital LatAm  
**Purpose:** Translate statistical results into actionable insights for financial literacy programme design

---

## Finding 1: Income Differences Across Latin American Countries

Median monthly income varies significantly across the six countries surveyed, ranging from $798 USD in Argentina to $1,458 USD in Brasil — a gap of nearly 83%. Chile and Brasil stand out as the highest-earning markets, while Argentina, Perú, and Colombia cluster at the lower end of the income spectrum. This disparity means that a "one-size-fits-all" financial literacy curriculum is unlikely to be effective across the region.

**Why it matters:** A programme designed around saving $200 per month may be realistic for a Brazilian respondent but entirely out of reach for someone in Argentina earning $766 on average. Programme content that ignores local income realities risks feeling irrelevant or discouraging to participants in lower-income countries.

**Recommendation:** Design country-specific financial targets and benchmarks. Modules on savings goals, emergency funds, and investment thresholds should be calibrated to local median income rather than a regional average, ensuring that recommendations feel achievable and contextually grounded for each national audience.

---

## Finding 2: The Relationship Between Age and Savings Behaviour

Respondents aged 29–32 save an average of $154 USD per month — more than double the $61 saved by the 18–22 age group — and their savings rate (15.5% of income) is nearly three times higher than that of the youngest group (5.7%). Since age does not predict income within this cohort (Finding 8, r = -0.029), the gap cannot be explained by older respondents simply earning more. The pattern — which holds both in absolute terms and as a percentage of earnings — points to savings behaviour genuinely improving with age and experience.

**Why it matters:** The youngest adults in the dataset are the group most likely to benefit from early financial education, yet they are currently saving the least. Intervening at ages 18–22 — before poor financial habits become entrenched — represents the highest-leverage opportunity for long-term impact.

**Recommendation:** Prioritise outreach to university students and early-career professionals aged 18–22. Programmes targeting this cohort should focus on building the savings habit from the first paycheck, using concrete and small initial goals (e.g., saving 5% of income) rather than aspirational targets that feel unreachable on an entry-level salary.

---

## Finding 3: Where the Biggest Expense Categories Are

Housing and food together consume more than half of respondents' monthly income on average — 28.5% and 23.8% respectively — leaving only 47.7% of income to cover transport, entertainment, education, healthcare, and savings combined. Healthcare is the smallest category at just 4.9%, while transport (10.1%) and entertainment (8.7%) are the areas where discretionary cuts are most feasible.

**Why it matters:** Financial literacy programmes often focus on discretionary spending (such as entertainment or dining out) as the primary lever for improving savings. However, when housing alone absorbs nearly a third of income, cutting entertainment entirely would recover only 8.7 percentage points — a modest gain that may not justify the behavioural effort required. Programmes that overlook the structural weight of fixed costs risk offering advice that is technically correct but practically ineffective.

**Recommendation:** Include a dedicated module on fixed-cost awareness and negotiation strategies — including how to evaluate rental agreements, understand utility contracts, and seek housing subsidies where available. Complement this with a realistic budgeting framework that acknowledges the limited flexibility of fixed expenses before addressing discretionary ones.

---

## Finding 4: How Credit Card Holders Differ from Non-Holders

Respondents who hold a credit card spend 16.1% more on food and 17.2% more on entertainment than those without one — yet they also save 6.7% more on average ($101.75 vs. $95.39 per month). Their income is only marginally higher (+1.5%), suggesting that the difference in spending is driven more by behaviour and access than by income level alone.

**Why it matters:** This finding challenges the assumption that credit cards are inherently harmful to savings. Credit card holders appear to both spend and save more — which may indicate greater financial engagement overall, or alternatively, that they are financing higher consumption through credit rather than cash. Without data on credit card debt repayment, both interpretations remain plausible and warrant attention.

**Recommendation:** Develop a dedicated module on responsible credit use that goes beyond warnings about debt. The module should cover how to use credit cards as a cash-flow management tool — including payment timing, reward optimisation, and the distinction between revolving debt and monthly settlement — so that participants can capture the benefits of credit access without falling into unsustainable spending patterns.

## Finding 5: The Relationship Between AI Tool Usage and Financial Satisfaction

Respondents who use AI tools for 11 or more hours per week report an average financial satisfaction score of 3.43 out of 5 — compared to 2.05 among those who use AI tools for 3 hours or fewer. This group also earns significantly more, with an average monthly income of $1,750 versus $747 for low users. The Pearson correlation between AI usage and financial satisfaction is r = 0.57 (p < 0.0001), indicating a statistically significant and moderately strong positive relationship.

**Why it matters:** While causality cannot be established from this data alone — higher earners may simply have more access to and motivation for using AI tools — the consistency of the pattern across income and satisfaction dimensions makes it a signal worth acting on. AI-assisted financial planning tools are becoming increasingly accessible, and there is a plausible mechanism by which they could help users make better-informed financial decisions.

**A note on the $1,750 figure:** This average appears to contradict Finding 9, which shows that no industry has a median income above $1,066. The two figures are compatible once the mechanism is understood. Finding 9 describes the *median* worker in each industry — the person in the middle of the distribution. High AI users, however, are not randomly distributed across the income range: chart 09 shows that the majority of orange dots (respondents using AI tools 11+ hours per week) sit above the median line of their respective industry box. In other words, high AI users are disproportionately drawn from the upper end of each industry's income distribution — the same high-earning individuals visible as outlier circles in chart 08. Their group average of $1,750 is not inconsistent with industry medians of $915–$1,066; it reflects the fact that within every sector, the highest earners are also the most likely to be intensive AI tool users.

**Recommendation:** Pilot an AI literacy component within the financial education curriculum, focused specifically on tools that support budgeting, expense tracking, and financial goal-setting. Frame AI not as a technical subject but as a practical resource, and evaluate whether participants who complete the module report higher financial confidence over a 6-month follow-up period.

## Finding 6: Housing Burden Differences by Country

Argentina carries the highest housing burden in the dataset, with respondents spending an average of 34.1% of their income on housing — nearly 10 percentage points more than Perú (24.6%), which has the lightest burden. Chile (32.6%) also faces a heavy housing cost relative to income. Notably, Argentina and Chile are not the lowest-income countries in the sample, yet they face some of the highest housing cost pressures, suggesting that the burden is driven by market conditions rather than income levels alone.

**Why it matters:** Housing is the single largest fixed expense across all six countries, and in markets like Argentina and Chile it leaves respondents with a structurally thin margin for saving or absorbing financial shocks. A programme that does not account for this reality will struggle to help participants in these markets meaningfully improve their financial position through behaviour change alone.

**Recommendation:** For programmes operating in Argentina and Chile, supplement the core financial literacy curriculum with country-specific content on housing cost management — including tenant rights, rental negotiation, shared housing arrangements, and government housing assistance schemes. In lower-burden countries such as Perú and Colombia, the freed-up margin should be explicitly reframed as a savings opportunity within the curriculum.

## Finding 7: Spending Patterns Across Age Groups (Supplementary)

When spending categories are plotted as a percentage of income across ages 18 to 32, the most striking observation is how stable the lines are. Housing consistently absorbs the largest share of income at every age, followed closely by food, and the remaining four categories — transport, entertainment, education, and healthcare — maintain relatively flat trajectories throughout the age range. No single category shows a dramatic rise or fall as respondents grow older.

**Why this matters:** The absence of a strong trend is itself a meaningful finding. It tells us that the savings gap between younger and older respondents (Finding 2) is not explained by a shift in spending priorities — 22-year-olds and 30-year-olds are allocating their income in roughly the same proportions across all categories. Combined with Finding 8, which shows that age does not predict income either (r = -0.029, p = 0.519), this leaves only one coherent explanation: the savings gap is purely behavioural. Older respondents in this age range do not earn more, and they do not spend less proportionally — they simply save more deliberately. The habit itself is the differentiator.

**Implication for programme design:** The target for intervention is the savings habit, not income level or spending behaviour. Since spending proportions are stable across ages and income does not rise with age within this cohort, there is no structural barrier preventing an 18-year-old from saving at the same rate as a 30-year-old. Programmes should focus on habit formation from the first paycheck — automating a fixed savings transfer before discretionary spending begins — rather than waiting for income to grow or attempting to restructure spending categories.

## Finding 8: Age Does Not Predict Income Within the 18–32 Range

A scatter plot of age against monthly income — with a linear trend line — reveals a Pearson correlation of r = -0.029 (p = 0.519), which is both negligibly small and statistically non-significant. The trend line is essentially flat, and the dispersion of data points is uniform across the entire 18–32 age range. In practical terms, knowing a respondent's age tells us almost nothing about what they earn.

**Why it matters:** This finding adds important nuance to Finding 2 (the savings gap between age groups). It is tempting to assume that older respondents save more simply because they earn more as they advance in their careers. Finding 8 shows that, within this age range, that assumption does not hold — incomes at age 30 are not systematically higher than incomes at age 20. The savings gap is therefore driven by behavioural and habitual differences, not by income growth with age. This shifts the focus of intervention: the problem is not that young adults lack income, but that they have not yet developed consistent savings habits.

**Recommendation:** Programme content for the 18–22 cohort should explicitly address the savings-habit gap rather than framing saving as something to do "once I earn more." Emphasising that income level at this age is roughly equivalent across the peer group — and that the difference between savers and non-savers is habit, not income — may increase motivation and reduce the perception that saving is only possible for higher earners.

## Finding 9: Industry Choice Has Less Impact on Income Than Expected

Median monthly income across the ten industries surveyed ranges from $915 (Marketing) to $1,066 (Recursos Humanos) — a spread of only $151, or roughly 16.5%. In other words, the ten industries represented in this dataset produce nearly identical median incomes for young professionals aged 18–32. However, the within-industry variance tells a very different story: minimum incomes reach as low as $300 across multiple sectors, while maximum values exceed $2,000 in Tecnología ($2,092), Finanzas ($2,874), Ingeniería ($2,524), and Educación ($2,296). The outlier circles visible in the chart represent these extreme earners — individuals whose incomes lie more than 1.5× IQR above the box.

**Why it matters:** For young adults considering industry choice as a primary strategy for increasing income, this data provides a cautionary signal. At the early-career stage (18–32), the industry one works in is a weaker predictor of income than within-industry factors such as country of residence, level of specialisation, or access to high-paying employers. The very wide income range visible within each box — and the presence of high-income outliers in nearly every sector — suggests that trajectory matters more than initial industry placement.

**Recommendation:** Financial literacy programmes should incorporate a session on income growth levers beyond industry selection — including skill specialisation, geographic mobility, negotiation, and transition to higher-paying roles within the same sector. Presenting the within-industry income range (e.g., "in Technology, you could earn anywhere from $300 to $2,092 depending on your role and country") can shift participants' mental model from passive acceptance of an "industry salary" to active management of career trajectory.

## Finding 10: High AI Users Are the Top Earners Within Every Industry

Chart 09 overlays the income distribution of high AI users (11+ hours/week, shown as orange dots) on top of the industry box plots from Finding 9. The visual pattern is striking: the large majority of orange dots sit above the median line of their respective industry box — and many cluster near the upper whisker or among the outlier circles. This is consistent across virtually every sector in the dataset, from Marketing to Finanzas.

This chart provides the visual mechanism behind two numbers that otherwise appear contradictory: the industry medians from Finding 9 ($915–$1,066) and the high AI user average from Finding 5 ($1,750). Both are accurate descriptions of the same dataset, seen from different angles. The industry median describes the typical worker; the $1,750 average describes a self-selected subgroup that disproportionately represents the top earners within each sector.

**Why it matters:** This pattern has two possible interpretations that carry very different implications for programme design. The first is that high AI tool usage causes higher income — perhaps by making workers more productive, better informed, or more capable of managing their finances and career strategically. The second is the reverse: high earners have more resources, more sophisticated work environments, and more motivation to use AI tools, and their income advantage predates their AI usage. The data cannot distinguish between these two explanations. What it does confirm is that the relationship between AI usage and income is not evenly distributed across the income spectrum — it is concentrated at the top.

**Recommendation:** This finding strengthens the case for piloting an AI literacy module (as recommended in Finding 5), but also adds a caution: simply introducing AI tools to participants will not automatically reproduce the outcomes seen in the high-usage group if the underlying driver is income rather than tool usage. The pilot should therefore be designed as a controlled experiment — measuring whether AI tool adoption among lower-income participants produces measurable improvements in financial outcomes over time — rather than assumed to be beneficial by association.

## Overall Conclusion

Taken together, the nine findings from this analysis converge on three higher-order insights that should shape the design of Futuro Digital LatAm's financial literacy programme.

**1. The savings gap is a habit gap, not an income gap.**
The most structurally important finding in this dataset is the combination of Findings 2, 7, and 8. Young adults aged 29–32 save nearly three times more of their income than those aged 18–22 — but this is not because they earn more (age does not predict income, r = -0.029) and not because they spend less proportionally (spending categories are stable across all ages). The only remaining explanation is behavioural: older respondents have developed a savings habit that younger ones have not yet formed. This reframes the programme's core mission. The obstacle is not structural scarcity — it is the absence of a deliberate savings routine at the start of adult financial life.

**2. Structural context sets the ceiling; behaviour determines how close to it people get.**
Findings 1, 3, and 6 establish that country of residence and housing costs create hard constraints on what is financially possible for each respondent. A participant in Argentina, where housing absorbs 34% of income and median earnings are $798/month, faces a fundamentally different equation than one in Brasil, where the median is $1,458 and housing takes 27%. No amount of budgeting advice closes an 83% income gap or negotiates away a structural housing market. Programmes must be country-calibrated, and in high-burden markets like Argentina and Chile they must acknowledge the limits of individual behaviour change before prescribing it.

**3. Financial engagement — not income, not industry, not age — is the strongest predictor of positive outcomes.**
Findings 4, 5, 9, and 10 point in the same direction. Credit card holders spend more but also save more — not because they earn significantly more (+1.5%), but because they appear to be more actively engaged with their finances. AI tool users report substantially higher financial satisfaction and income, and chart 09 (Finding 10) shows visually that these high AI users are concentrated at the top of the income distribution within every industry — not spread evenly across it. Within every sector, the gap between the lowest and highest earners dwarfs the gap between industries, suggesting that how actively someone manages their career and finances matters far more than which sector they chose. The common thread across all of these patterns is engagement: people who interact more deliberately with financial tools, information, and decisions tend to come out ahead. This is the behaviour that a financial literacy programme is best positioned to cultivate — with the important caveat, raised in Finding 10, that the direction of causality between AI usage and income remains unconfirmed and warrants a controlled pilot before being assumed.
